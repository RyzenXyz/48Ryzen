LAW 1 • Strategy & Influence

Never Outshine the Master

Always make those above you feel comfortably superior. In your desire to please or impress them, do not go too far in displaying your talents or you might accomplish the opposite—inspire fear and insecurity.

The Principle

Make your masters appear more brilliant than they are and you will attain the heights of power. Everyone has insecurities. When you show yourself in the presence of a person in power to be more intelligent, witty, or charming than they are, you are inadvertently making them feel that they are inferior.

Historical Example

Nicolas Fouquet, finance minister to Louis XIV, threw a lavish party at his château that outshone Versailles. The display of wealth and taste exceeded that of the king himself, leading to Fouquet's imprisonment and disgrace.

Key Points

  • Display your talents subtly
  • Make superiors feel secure
  • Avoid threatening their position
  • Enhance their reputation

About Robert Greene

Robert Greene is an American author known for his books on strategy, power, and seduction. His work draws from philosophy, history, and psychology to provide insights into human nature and the dynamics of power.

The 48 Laws of Power has become a modern classic, influencing leaders, entrepreneurs, and thinkers across various fields. Greene's ability to distill complex historical patterns into actionable principles has made this work indispensable for understanding power dynamics.
